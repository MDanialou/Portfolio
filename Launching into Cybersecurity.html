As one of the nine pillars of technologies driving the 4th Industrial Revolution (BCG Middle East - EN, N.D.), Cybersecurity is a domain that requires special attention in this digital age. “It is an evolving discipline which requires constant research to be able to keep up with upcoming threats as well as developing effective methods and approaches to match these threats.” (Forum W.E., N.D.) In Unit 2 and 3, we reviewed some important considerations to cybersecurity which organisations should consider to achieve a good cybersecurity posture:

• Good security practices to generate, process and store data by ensuring data Confidentiality, Integrity and Availability.

• Good security measures to predict, protect, detect and response to security incidents with qualified workforce in formal roles.

• Design of secure systems to ensure penetration and disruption are difficult and compromise is easy to detect.

• The implementation of adequate security countermeasures to strengthen access to systems with a formal training program for users to detect and report cyberthreats.

• The design of a secure network to prevent unauthorised access, misuse, modification, denial of service etc.

• Adoption of a threat modelling technique to help identify threats that may impact systems.

• Implementation of a secure design.

UML diagrams

The emphasis is on the practical application of UML diagrams in designing software that mitigates threats and vulnerabilities. Examples of UML diagrams are Class, Abuse, Sequence etc.

Class diagram: A class diagram in the Unified Modeling Language (UML) is a type of static structure diagram that describes the structure of a system by showing the system's: classes, their attributes, operations (or methods), and the relationships among objects.

Use case Diagram: This diagram is used to capture the dynamic aspect of a system, gather the requirements of a system, get an outside view of a system, Identify the external and internal factors influencing the system, and show the interaction among the requirements are actors.

Sequence Diagram: Is an interaction diagram that details how operations are carried out -- what messages are sent and when.

Reference: www.visual-paradigm.com. (n.d.). FREE Learning Resources: UML, Agile, TOGAF, PMBOK, BPMN. [online] Available at: https://www.visual-paradigm.com/guide/.

DISCUSSION 2: Initial Post

My initial post identified 2 network security solutions that have changed the cybersecurity landscape. These are the Advanced Malware analysis Sandbox solution and the Next Generation Firewall (NGFW). The Advanced Malware analysis Sandbox solution has for instance enabled organisations to detect previously undetected advanced malware such as Advanced Presistemt Threats (APT). By moving detection from appearance of malware to behaviour, the Advanced Malware analysis Sandbox solution has provided organisations with a powerful and novel way to detect advanced malware and collect intelligence to profile their adversaries. The Next Generation Firewall has also taken the traditional packet filtering firewall to a new level by combining advanced features such as deep packet inspection with application control and user awareness to detect malware and safeguard organisation’s critical assets and data. The following is an excerpt of the initial post. The sophistication of cyber-attacks and their increased frequency have rendered traditional malware detection technologies which rely on signatures and known threats ineffective and helpless (Karantzas & Patsakis, 2021). New technologies such as advanced malware analysis sandbox and Next Generation firewall (NGFW) have provided deep insight into malware behaviour against systems and applications and enabled organisations to detect unknown and zero-day malware by dynamically analysing and automating the malware analysis process to improve efficiency (FireEye, N.D.).

The following is the summary post after discussion and feedback of my peers.

The sophistication of cyber-attacks and their increased frequency have rendered traditional malware detection technologies ineffective and helpless (Karantzas and Patsakis, 2021). Consequently, an effective malware detection can no longer be reliant on appearance alone. My initial post focused on 2 technologies that are the advanced malware analysis sandbox and the Next Generation Firewall (NGFW). The advanced malware analysis technology has enabled organisations to isolate and analyse malware in a safe and closed virtual environment, to inventory the behaviour of malware and gather well-needed intelligence. The technology has helped detect Advanced Persistent threats (APTs) which otherwise would have compromised organisations without their knowledge. (FireEye, N.D.) Liana (2021) has argued that there is no known method to defend against zero-day attacks which I could not agree with. This is because malware detection and defenses and particularly of zero-day attacks rely on a combination of effective security controls such as: secure architecture, network monitoring and defense, software assets inventory controls etc.. which many organisations are lacking. I concur with Samuel (2022) on the malware analysis types and especially the dynamic analysis that allows activities of a suspicious file to be observed which shows the usefulness of sandbox technology. The Next Generation Firewall (NGFW), ability to inspect traffic based on IPs and ports, to decrypt and perform deep packet analysis are unrivaled. Its advanced capabilities such as application awareness and user control (Check Point Software, N.D.) are critical to detecting advanced malware evasion techniques. In conclusion, the advanced malware analysis sandbox and the Next Generation Firewall technologies have revolutionized malware detection and provided deep insight into malware behaviour. However, these technologies are not a remedy for all advanced cyber-threats (Mills & Legg, 2020). Additionally, their advanced capabilities may affect applications and user experience due to performance degradation and latency. Organisations must therefore decide where to implement them and the features that must be enabled to detect and mitigate advanced cyber-threats.
